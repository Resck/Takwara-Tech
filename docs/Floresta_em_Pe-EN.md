## **Executive Summary**

This report presents a critical analysis and source validation of the presentations given at the panel "Challenges for Maintaining the Standing Forest," held on November 15, 2025, at the Federal University of Par√° (UFPA), as part of the COP30 events in Bel√©m. The objective of this document is to correct the "shortcomings"‚Äîincomplete information, unattributed data, and missing bibliographic references‚Äîfrom the official event transcript 1, supplementing and validating the speakers' claims through external academic, institutional, and legal sources.

The analysis of the panel, which brought together seven experts from the fields of soil science, economics, carbon finance, and land governance, reveals a profound dichotomy that defines the current state of the climate debate in Brazil.

On one hand, the panel demonstrated robust scientific advances in the ability to value the forest. This includes:

1. A new carbon quantification methodology (UNESP/University of Sassari) that proves more accurate than IPCC standards by correcting significant underestimations in soil and leaf litter.2  
2. An economic paradigm (UFPA/NAEA) for the "bioeconomy of socio-biodiversity," which values traditional knowledge and diversity management in direct opposition to industrial homogenization ("A√ßa√≠-ization").4  
3. The centrality of "soil health" as a pillar for the climate resilience of agriculture, arguing for the interdependence between agricultural production and the standing forest.7

On the other hand, the panel exposed critical governance and market failures that fundamentally undermine these advances. The discussions revealed:

1. Serious allegations of socio-environmental conflicts in carbon projects in Par√° (municipalities of Breves and Portel), where companies (like Eco Mapu√°) use the Rural Environmental Registry (CAR) to legitimize the sale of credits over the territories of traditional communities (RESEX and settlements) without prior consultation, a phenomenon conceptualized as the "agrarian metaverse" and "carbon neocolonialism."9  
2. A systemic "profound land tenure insecurity," marked by 50 million hectares of undesignated public lands in the Amazon, which become the main vector for "land grabbing" and "green grabbing."1  
3. The creation of legal structures, such as the Fiagro Law 13, and public-private business models, like Par√°'s agreement with the LEAF Coalition via CAAP S.A. 14, which, in the absence of effective territorial governance, raise serious questions about sovereignty, benefit-sharing, and carbon pricing.1

The main challenge identified by the panel is not a lack of science to value the forest, nor a lack of capital to finance it. The central challenge is the structural failure of the State to establish transparent and effective land governance, allowing market instruments to operate in a regulatory vacuum that threatens the rights of communities and the very integrity of the ecosystem services they propose to save.

## **II. Analysis of Presentation 1: Prof. Ira√™ (UNESP)**

**Presentation Title:** "Carbon Quantification and Ecosystem Services Aiming at Forest Preservation."1

Discourse Analysis:  
Prof. Ira√™ structured his speech in two parts: a climate contextualization (filling in for Prof. Artacho) and the presentation of his core research.

1. **Climate Context:**  
   * **Claim 1.1:** The speaker cited an "article in Nature in 2021" 1 that mapped the temperature increase over the last 24,000 years, with an abrupt acceleration in the last 6,000 years.  
   * **Claim 1.2:** He cited data from the Astronomical and Geological Institute (IAG) of USP 1 on the increase in extreme events in S√£o Paulo.  
   * **Claim 1.3:** He referenced a "work by Professor Lau" 1 that classifies the planet's main carbon reservoirs in the order: 1st Ocean, 2nd Soil, 3rd Vegetation.  
2. The UNESP/Sassari Methodology:  
   The core of the presentation was a critique of the standard carbon quantification methodology recommended by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), and the presentation of an alternative methodology, developed in partnership with the University of Sassari (Italy) since 2015.1  
   * **Critique 1 (Soil Depth):** The IPCC suggests soil sampling to a minimum depth of 30 cm.1 The speaker demonstrated, based on a doctoral thesis (student Deise) in the Atlantic Forest (Botucatu), that this depth is insufficient. The study found 74 tons of carbon/hectare (ton/ha) from 0-30 cm, but an additional 76 ton/ha from 30-100 cm. The standard methodology, therefore, ignored more than 50% of the soil carbon.1  
   * **Critique 2 (Analysis Method):** The standard method for fertility (Walkley-Black) 1 underestimated carbon. By using the Elemental Analyzer (CHN), which burns the sample and measures total carbon, the result jumped from 129 ton/ha to 181 ton/ha‚Äîan increase of 40%.1  
   * **Critique 3 (Leaf Litter):** The IPCC suggests a 50% conversion factor (dry weight to carbon).1 The CHN analysis in the Botucatu forests revealed a content of only 38%, an overestimation of 24% by the IPCC method.1 The speaker emphasized that the goal is not to inflate numbers, but to be "fair and accurate."1  
   * **Research Validation:** The speaker stated that this work was published in the journal *Scientific Reports*.1  
   * **Application in the Amazon:** The methodology was applied in Acar√° (PA), in partnership with the Piabiru Institute.1 The results in the Amazon were even more discrepant: 211 ton/ha (UNESP/CHN method) versus 111 ton/ha (IPCC method), an increase of 90%.1

**Correction of Shortcomings and External Validation:**

* **Reference 1.1 (Nature 2021):** The claim 1 refers to the study by Osman et al. (2021), published in *Nature*. The research used climate models to reconstruct global temperatures since the Last Glacial Maximum (24,000 years ago), confirming that the magnitude and rate of modern anthropogenic warming are "unprecedented."16  
* **Reference 1.3 (Prof. Lau):** The claim 1 about carbon reservoirs (Ocean \> Soil \> Vegetation) is a scientific consensus widely documented in the IPCC reports themselves, although the identity of "Professor Lau" could not be confirmed by the sources.  
* **Methodology Reference (Scientific Reports):** The claim 1 is validated. Articles 2 confirm the publication of the research led by Prof. Ira√™ Guerrini 3 in the journal *Scientific Reports* (of the *Nature* group). The reports confirm the presentation's points: the critique of the IPCC's 30 cm depth (insufficient for tropical soils) and the proposal to extend it to 1 meter, in addition to the use of more precise equipment (elemental analyzer) to correct the underestimation of carbon.2  
* **Project Reference (Acar√°):** The claim 1 is validated. The "Casa da Floresta" (House of the Forest) 21 is the initiative of the UNESP-Instituto Piabiru partnership in the municipality of Acar√° (PA), serving as a reference center for social technologies and a UNESP research base in the Amazon.24

## **III. Analysis of Presentation 2: Prof. Danilo Fernandes (UFPA/NAEA)**

**Presentation Title:** "The Bioeconomy of Socio-biodiversity and its Importance for the Preservation and Conservation of Tropical Forests."1

Discourse Analysis:  
Prof. Danilo Fernandes, an economist from the Center for Advanced Amazonian Studies (NAEA) at UFPA 1, presented a theoretical and empirical analysis of development models in the Amazon.

1. **The Dichotomy of Paradigms:** The presentation contrasted two productive logics:  
   * **Mechanical-Chemical (Industrialist) Paradigm:** Treats nature as "dead nature."1 Its logic is standardization and homogenization for economies of scale (e.g., monocultures).1  
   * **Organic Paradigm (Biome Bioeconomy):** Treats nature as "living nature."1 Its logic is the management of diversity, based on "traditional (tacit) knowledge" and "endogenous knowledge."1 It is defined as the "bioeconomy of socio-biodiversity."1  
2. **Distinct Economic Rationales:** The speaker argued that the agents of these paradigms are different and cannot be modeled in the same way.  
   * **Example of A√ßa√≠:** A family producer of a√ßa√≠, upon reaching a good price, stops harvesting, as their goal is "well-being" and optimizing family labor.1 A businessman, in the same situation, increases production to "maximize profit."1  
3. **Measuring the (Hidden) Bioeconomy:** The speaker stated that the economy of socio-biodiversity is "underreported."1  
   * **Claim 2.1:** He cited "Professor Francisco de Assis Costa" 1 as the leader at NAEA in developing methodologies (input-output matrices) to estimate the real size of this economy.  
   * **Claim 2.2 (Data):** Based on these matrices, he estimated the GDP of the socio-biodiversity bioeconomy of Par√° at R$ 7 billion.1 He stated that the export market (e.g., a√ßa√≠) is small, about 3% 1, with the regional and national markets being the main drivers.  
   * **Claim 2.3 (The Threat of "A√ßa√≠-ization"):** The speaker warned 1 that pressure from the international market (the 3%) for scale is forcing the "homogenization of floodplain areas."1 A√ßa√≠, a product of the Organic Paradigm, risks being captured by the Mechanical-Chemical Paradigm, becoming a commodity and generating negative impacts, despite being a "forest product."1  
   * **Claim 2.4 (Dynamics):** Data from 20 years show that trajectories T1/T2 (peasant/diversity) grew by 4.2%, while T7 (employer/grains) grew by 9.7%.1 The conclusion is that systems that conserve do grow, but they lose relative space to homogeneous systems.

**Correction of Shortcomings and External Validation:**

* **Reference 2.1 (Prof. Francisco de Assis Costa):** The claim 1 is central and validated. Prof. Francisco de Assis Costa is a Full Professor at NAEA/UFPA 26 and the main academic reference for the input-output matrix methodology to measure the "Bioeconomy of socio-biodiversity in the state of Par√°."5 His work substantiates the theoretical distinction between the agro-industrialist bioeconomy (mechanical-chemical) and the "bio-ecological bioeconomy" (organic) 4, as explained by Prof. Danilo.

## **V. Analysis of Presentation 4: Prof. Ant√¥nio Ganga (University of Sassari)**

**Presentation Title:** "Evaluation of the physicochemical influence of soil physicochemical properties and land use history on the carbon stock in organic soils."1

Discourse Analysis:  
Prof. Ant√¥nio Ganga presented a case study from Sardinia (Italy), reinforcing the 10-year international collaboration 1 mentioned by Prof. Ira√™.

1. **Context (Europe):** The research is part of the "European Soil Strategy for 2030" 1, which aims to improve soil quality on the continent.  
2. **Methodology (Sardinia):** The study compared carbon stocks in two distinct pedosystems (geological substrates) 1:  
   * **Site 1 (Monti):** On intrusive rock (granite), resulting in acidic soils (Cambisols).1  
   * **Site 2 (Santa Maria da Palma):** On limestone rock (limestone/loam), resulting in Luvisols.1

   * Natural forests (potential vegetation) of *Quercus* (e.g., *Quercus ilex* and *Quercus suber* \- Cork Oak).1

4. **Results:** The speaker presented statistical analyses (PFA and PCA) 1 indicating that, although forests have a higher soil organic carbon (SOC) stock 1, the overall system behavior is strongly dominated by soil texture (derived from the parent rock) and chemical properties (like pH) altered by human management.1  
5. **Status:** The speaker concluded by stating that "the manuscript on this work is under review."1

**Correction of Shortcomings and External Validation:**

* **Research Line Reference:** Although the specific work presented 1 is under review, the sources 32 confirm that Prof. Antonio Ganga and his team at the University of Sassari have a robust history of publications on the topic.
